          <td style="border: 1px double #808080; padding: 0.05cm"

            <p>Launched on 13 Dec 1964 as the second operational <a
                href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_%28satellite%29"
                target="_blank">TRANSIT</a> spacecraft, it should be
              more correctly called OSCAR -2, O-2 or NSS 30020. <br>
              Unfortunately, the navigation system failed within 2 weeks
              of launch. <br>
              The <a href="/doc/1964-83D.pdf?raw=true" target="_blank">telemetry
                beacon</a> can be CW or modulated depending on solar
              lighting conditions. </p>
            <p>The PAM data is 35 steps with a repeat cycle of about
              12.5 seconds. <br>
              Each step has a duty cycle of about 75% data value and 25%
              return to centre frequency value. <br>
              These steps vary a 5400 Hz centre frequency sub carrier
              oscillator, IRIG 10, that then frequency modulates the RF
              carrier. <br>
              Another SCO at 10.5 kHz, IRIG 12,&nbsp; is sometimes
              present. <br>
              It has a pattern of five pulses with gaps, followed by a
              longer gap at about a 1.5 Hz rate. </p>
            <p>A SSB receiver will resolve, separately, the carrier as a
              single tone and each of the sidebands as "musical" tones.<br>
              Radiation satellite, Studied magnetic field, celestial
              field, ultraviolet data. <br>
              This satellite is still transmitting and thus is probably
              the oldest satellite still transmitting. The sequence of
              tones has speeded up quite considerably over time.. </p>
            <p>Also see Maik Hermenau's TRANSIT 5B5 <a

    <span style="font-weight: bold; text-decoration: underline;">Introduction<br>
      <br>
    </span>During weathersat reception of NOAA/Meteor on the 137 MHz
    band i did see quite often unidentified interrupting signals. <br>
    After a bit of Googling around I learned that these were Dead
    Satellite?s that still are broadcasting given the right sunlight
    conditions, the signal strength is sufficient to show up in
    SDRSharp, not all signals are very strong some very weak and needed
    a good close up zoom in SDRSharp. <br>
    <br>
    There are many unused Satellite?s who apparently having a life of
    their own varying from Military, Navigation, Experimental, Weather,
    and also Amateur ones. <br>
    Most are not transmitting any usable telemetry or weather images but
    they still use the original frequencies to sent out a unmodulated
    carrier, which interferes with the current operational Satellite?s
    in the 136/138 MHz band. Normally when a satellite goes out of
    service and runs almost out of fuel the last reserve is used to
    shift it in a graveyard orbit which resides above the normal
    operation orbit, it will be switched off and left on their own.<br>
    &nbsp;<br>
    But this will not always goes as planned, sometimes due solar
    radiation or other technical failures it will not respond again to
    the ground station commands, which leaves the Satellite in its
    current orbit. <br>
    Some have a timer onboard which will be activated at end of life
    scheduled to end/shortcut the power feed to the transmitter so it
    will not broadcast again, also this can fail. <br>
    Until some day they might not work at all anymore.. <br>
